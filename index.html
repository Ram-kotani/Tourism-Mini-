Explore the Beautiful Ilūkste

Your guide to the peaceful city center, its monuments, and rich history.

Ilūkste attracts with its peaceful city center, where in the warm season, a fountain sparkles with water surrounded by churches. In the city center, you can visit three churches of different denominations, the former Jesuit monastery complex with fragments of the Church, the sculpture of the Virgin Mary and the Horse sculpture. The Ilūkste River flows through Ilūkste. You can learn more about the history of the city and Sēlija by visiting the “Sēļu istaba” (Sēļu Room) of the Children and Youth Center. In the Sports Center of the Augšdaugava District Sports School, you can enjoy water sports all year round, warm up in the sauna and visit the salt room and gym. Families with children have access to a spacious and comfortable Children's Recreation and Playground. In the summer season, you are welcome to enjoy fun events and concerts at the Ilūkste Open-Air Stage.

Water Fountain in the City Center

During the summer season, a water fountain sparkles in the city center, surrounded by a century-old oak tree that tells the story of the city's history.

Statue of the Virgin Mary

Located near the Roman Catholic Church of the Blessed Virgin Mary, this sculpture was unveiled in 2019 and created by sculptor Tālivaldis Muzikants. It symbolizes the life-giving water and the lily flower, representing Mary.

View Image

Horse Sculpture

Erected in 2018 during the Ilūkste regional festival, this horse sculpture honors the horse depicted in the city's coat of arms. It symbolizes Ilūkste and is featured on souvenirs and representative materials.

View Image

Former Jesuit Monastery with Church Fragments

Once the largest Catholic Church in the Baltic region, the Jesuit monastery complex in Ilūkste holds a rich history, with parts of the original church still standing today. The site has been included in the State List of Protected Cultural Monuments since 2021.

View Image

Rainis Monument

Unveiled in 1990, the Rainis Monument honors the Latvian poet and playwright who attended the opening of the local secondary school in 1927. Sculptor Indulis Folkmanis created this monument in his honor.
